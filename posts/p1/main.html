Could we build a time machine?

Daniel Beall

March 4th, 2017

1 Introduction

To the best of our knowledge, traveling backwards in time is impossible. But, with our current physics knowledge, it is a possibility that we could travel to the future. By Einstein’s special relativity, it’s a physical law, albeit a somewhat unintuitive one, that time passes differently for different observers depending on their speeds. Furthermore, this time dilation has been validated experimentally. A clock moving at extreme speed experiences less time compared to one that is stationary. We can model time dilation with some fairly simple math.

We call T0 the time in the moving frame of reference and T time time in the stationary frame of reference. Gamma is commonly referred to as the Lorentz factor. For a quick example of the above equations, say we are moving at 0.9c or ninety percent of the speed of light relative to a stationary observer. Then, plugging in our 0.9c we find T = 2.30T0. In other words, for every second that passes in the moving frame of reference, 2.3 seconds pass in the outside frame of reference. Now the question if if could we use this principle to engineer a device, with our current technology, to allow a person to travel to the future.

2 The Time Machine

Imagine we have an eccentric billionaire that wants to travel to the future. Maybe they are tired of living in 2017 and want to travel to 2027. They want to spend a year in the time machine while ten years pass on the outside. In other words, we have a Lorentz factor of 10. A current technology that allows us to reach relativistic velocities is the particle accelerator. We use magnetic fields to confine particles and accelerate them to a speeds near the speed of light. Imagine we could accelerate a person in a pod, our eccentric billionaire, along a circular track to those relativistic velocities. This ’track’ would be inside a vacuum tunnel to remove any friction. The pod would be accelerated in a manner similar to a maglev train. For a Lorentz factor of 10 we must accelerate the the pod to roughly 0.995c. This will be a part of the downfall of the eccentric billionaire’s plan. Just to accelerate the pod for the required 0.995c will require huge amounts of energy.

This formula is the kinetic energy of the pod. Let’s assume that our billionaire’s pod weighs around 3000kg. The billionaire needs some food, water, and also likes to ride in comfort. The weight is also similar to proposed Hyperloop pods. So, plugging in our mass and Lorentz factor into the equation, we calculate that the pod, when accelerated, will have a kinetic energy of 2.42 × 1021 J or roughly 4 times the world’s energy consumption per year. This does not take into account inefficiencies in energy conversion and transfer. There is another pressing issue that puts a bigger stopper to this concept. Even if the eccentric billionaire could buy enough energy to power his machine he wouldn’t be able to survive it. The centripetal acceleration would kill him.

Say, we had a circular track with a radius of 10km. The centripetal acceleration experienced by the person in the pod, at full speed, would be approximately 9 × 1012 g. For reasonable centripetal forces, the radius would be several light-years, which is obviously completely unreasonable given current technology.

3 Further Thoughts

In conclusion, with our current knowledge of physics and engineering it’s not possible to build a time machine. Maybe the eccentric trillionaire of the 23rd century will be able to do so. Realistically, we’re going to need a spacecraft that can make several light-year journeys at extreme speeds.
